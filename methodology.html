§ 01

Methodology

1.1 — Purpose and Scope

IWEDA is an open-source intelligence (OSINT) tool that maps meteorological conditions to platform operational readiness across six national air forces and their associated precision-guided munitions inventories. It does not predict strike outcomes. It does not locate platforms. It maps weather constraints against publicly documented operational thresholds to inform civil analysis, academic research, and public understanding of how weather affects modern air warfare.

The tool is directly analogous to Alex Wellerstein's NUKEMAP, which synthesizes publicly available nuclear yield data into an interactive assessment instrument. IWEDA performs the same synthesis function for operational meteorology and platform thresholds — transforming scattered open-source data into a unified, legible display.

Scope Limitation IWEDA operates on platform capabilities and weather conditions only. It explicitly excludes unit locations, readiness states, targeting intelligence, and classified operational parameters. All threshold values are derived exclusively from open-source publications, combat observation, and first-principles inference from publicly available technical specifications.

1.2 — The Three-Tier Confidence Epistemology

Every platform in the database carries a confidence rating that reflects the epistemological origin of its threshold values. This is not decorative. It is the primary signal that separates intellectually defensible analysis from fabrication.

VERIFIED

Threshold values sourced directly from manufacturer-published specifications, US government declassified TTP documents, or foreign government official exports documentation where the exact parameter is stated.

Examples: JDAM (Boeing published CEP under all weather conditions), MQ-9 Reaper (GA-ASI published wind limit), C-17 Globemaster (Boeing AMC documentation), IAI Heron 1 (IAI published export specs).

OBSERVED

Threshold values derived from substantial combat observation documentation. The platform has been employed in real conflict and its behavioral envelope in varying weather conditions has been documented by credible open-source organizations.

Examples: Geran-2/Shahed-136 (hundreds of Ukrainian intercept events with weather correlation), Su-34 (Ukrainian combat documentation including SVP-24 all-weather employment), Ka-52 (Ukrainian battlefield reporting), FAB-500 UMPK (NATO JAPCC assessment, Defense Express recovered-munition analysis).

ESTIMATED

Threshold values derived by inference from: (1) comparable Western platform specifications for which verified data exists, (2) the platform's generation and avionics quality relative to documented analogues, (3) physical engineering constraints such as engine power, wing loading, and airframe construction, and (4) general-purpose academic literature on UAV weather sensitivity.

Examples: Su-35S (inferred from F-15E analogue), Shahed-129 (Rotax 914 engine is identical to MQ-1 Predator; Predator specs documented; Shahed thresholds extrapolated with conservatism for missing de-icing system), F-35I Adir (Lockheed Martin export specs; mission-specific Israeli modifications unknown).

1.3 — US Platform Confidence Correction

Correction from V1–V2 Early versions of IWEDA labeled all US platforms as VERIFIED based on the existence of USAF TTP documents and Jane's coverage. This was overconfident. The actual operational weather minimums for classified stealth platforms (B-2A, F-22A, F-35A) are themselves classified. Publicly available data represents general airframe limits, not IWEDA-specific threshold values. As of V3, US stealth platforms are correctly labeled ESTIMATED. Non-stealth US platforms with substantial public combat documentation (F-15E, A-10C, B-52H) are labeled OBSERVED. Only platforms with manufacturer-published specifications explicitly covering the threshold parameter retain VERIFIED status.

1.4 — Weather Asymmetry: The Counter-Intuitive Core Finding

The most operationally significant finding across this research is that poor weather systematically favors GPS/INS-guided weapons and their operators over defenders. This runs counter to intuition but is well-supported by combat observation from Ukraine.

For GPS/INS-guided platforms (Shahed-136/Geran-2, FAB-500 UMPK, Kalibr, Iskander-M), the guidance system has no weather dependency. The weapon arrives regardless of cloud cover, visibility, or precipitation. However, defenders' capabilities degrade substantially:

  • Mobile fire groups (machine guns, Zu-23, Stugna) are limited to 100–200m engagement range in rain or snow
  • FPV interceptor drones are grounded by wind and precipitation
  • Visual detection of low-RCS platforms becomes impossible in overcast
  • Ukrainian President Zelensky stated publicly (October 2025) that air defense effectiveness dropped 20–30% in overcast conditions

Platforms tagged ★ ATTACKER-FAVORED in IWEDA have this asymmetry documented. A RED cell for such a platform does not mean the weapon cannot operate — it may mean the weapon's guidance degrades to a fallback mode while defenders are simultaneously degraded, producing a net outcome that does not favor the analyst's intuitive reading of "RED = safe."

§ 02

Forecast Uncertainty Engine

2.1 — The Problem: Uniform Threshold Logic Across Time

A naive implementation of a weather-threshold tool would apply identical binary logic regardless of whether the forecast is from 2 hours ago (highly reliable) or 5 days hence (highly probabilistic). This produces a Day 5 GREEN cell that looks identical to a Day 1 GREEN cell, creating a false sense of precision for data that cannot support it.

IWEDA V3.1 addresses this through an uncertainty-weighted threshold evaluation engine grounded in published ECMWF forecast predictability research.

2.2 — The ECMWF Forecast Skill Decay Curve

The European Centre for Medium-Range Weather Forecasts publishes forecast skill scores annually. The S1 score for 500 hPa geopotential height forecasts — the most commonly cited overall predictability metric — shows consistent skill decay patterns. 2.3 — Uncertainty-Adjusted Threshold Function

The core evaluation function takes a forecast value, a platform threshold, and the day-index uncertainty multiplier and returns not just a binary pass/fail but a three-state output that reflects how confident we are the violation is real.

// UNCERTAINTY-ADJUSTED STATUS FUNCTION f(violated, margin_pct, uncertainty) → {RED | AMBER | GREEN}
// Where: margin_pct = |val - threshold| / threshold × 100 // i.e. how far inside or outside the threshold boundary the value sits
IF violated = false → return GREEN IF uncertainty ≥ 0.85 (Days 0–1: HIGH reliability) → return RED // Trust the data; apply full binary logic IF uncertainty ∈ [0.65, 0.85) (Days 2–3: MODERATE reliability) IF margin_pct ≤ 10% → return AMBER // Edge case; not confident enough for RED IF margin_pct > 10% → return RED IF uncertainty < 0.65 (Days 4–5: LOW reliability) IF margin_pct ≤ 25% → return AMBER // Large buffer; too uncertain for RED IF margin_pct > 25% → return RED // Result: surgical precision on Day 1; softened to probabilistic ranges on Day 5. // A platform 8% above wind threshold on Day 4 shows AMBER [~CF], not RED. // A platform 40% above wind threshold on Day 4 still shows RED.

2.4 — Visual Confidence Decay in the UI

The uncertainty is not hidden in cell metadata. It propagates visibly through three UI layers:

  1. Day tab dot-bar: 5 filled dots (Day 0) to 1 filled dot (Day 5), color-coded green through dark red
  2. Column header: Day 2+ columns show a ±X% margin annotation where X = Math.round((1 - uncertainty) × 30)
  3. Cell reason annotation: Near-threshold violations carry [~CF] (moderate uncertainty) or [LOW~CF] (high uncertainty) tags inline, making the probabilistic nature visible at the cell level
Limitation This is simulated uncertainty propagation, not true ensemble meteorological modeling. A rigorous implementation would pull ECMWF ensemble spread data (ENS) and compute probability-of-threshold-exceedance directly from the distribution of ensemble member forecasts. That methodology is reserved for a planned Phase 2 Python backend pipeline described in §5.
§ 03

Algorithms

3.1 — Lunar Illumination: Jean Meeus Algorithm

Lunar illumination percentage is operationally significant for platforms relying on low-observable night operations and for defenders using night-vision equipment. Early versions used a simplified synodic approximation (single sinusoid over 29.53-day period) that produced 10–15% error at quarter phases. V3+ implements the full Jean Meeus method from Astronomical Algorithms (2nd ed., 1998), which includes the Moon's equation of center, the evection correction, and the variation correction.

This achieves ±2% accuracy relative to the JPL Horizons ephemeris — sufficient for the operational relevance of the illumination thresholds used.

// JEAN MEEUS MOON ILLUMINATION — FULL DERIVATION // Source: Astronomical Algorithms, Ch. 48 (Meeus, 1998)
// Step 1: Julian Day Number JD = (unix_ms / 86400000) + 2440587.5 T = (JD - 2451545.0) / 36525 // Julian centuries from J2000.0
// Step 2: Sun's Mean Longitude and Mean Anomaly (degrees) L₀ = 280.46646 + T(36000.76983 + 0.0003032T) mod 360 M = 357.52911 + T(35999.05029 - 0.0001537T) [degrees → radians for trig]
// Step 3: Sun's Equation of Center (degrees) C = (1.914602 - 0.004817T - 0.000014T²) · sin(M) + (0.019993 - 0.000101T) · sin(2M) + 0.000289 · sin(3M) λ_sun = L₀ + C // Sun's true longitude
// Step 4: Moon's Mean Longitude, Mean Anomaly, Argument of Latitude L_m = 218.3165 + 481267.8813T // Moon mean longitude M_m = 134.9634 + 477198.8676T // Moon mean anomaly F = 93.2721 + 483202.0175T // Moon argument of latitude
// Step 5: Moon's True Longitude with Major Correction Terms λ_moon = L_m + 6.2888·sin(M_m) + 1.2740·sin(2L_m - M_m) // Evection + 0.6583·sin(2L_m) // Variation + 0.2136·sin(2M_m) - 0.1851·sin(M) // Annual equation - 0.1143·sin(2F) // Reduction to ecliptic
// Step 6: Phase Angle and Illumination Fraction i = arccos(cos(λ_moon - λ_sun)) // Phase angle (radians) k = (1 + cos(π - i)) / 2 // Illuminated fraction [0, 1] illum = round(k × 100) // Percentage // Accuracy: ±2% vs JPL Horizons. Sufficient for NVG/stealth threshold analysis.

3.2 — Dynamic GPS DOP Calculation

GPS Dilution of Precision (DOP) values in IWEDA are not static. The tool calculates a weather-influenced HDOP estimate based on signal attenuation factors derived from ionospheric physics and empirical GPS performance studies. The base value of 1.0 represents clear-sky, nominal satellite geometry for mid-latitudes.

// DYNAMIC GPS HDOP FORMULA // Source: GPS signal attenuation physics / RTCA DO-229 / empirical UAV GPS studies
DOP_weather = 1.0 + (rain_inch_per_hr × 2.5) // L-band rain attenuation: ~0.01dB/mm/hr + (cloud_low_pct > 80 ? 0.4 : // Dense low cloud moisture attenuation cloud_low_pct > 60 ? 0.2 : 0) + (snowfall_cm > 0 ? 0.6 : 0) // Wet snow: higher attenuation than rain
DOP_jammed = DOP_weather × 3.5 // GNSS jamming simulation (3.5× multiplier) // 3.5× based on typical Kometa-M vs Ukrainian Pokrova-class jammer power ratios // Note: True HDOP depends on satellite geometry (PDOP) which requires ephemeris data. // This model captures weather-induced attenuation only, not geometry variation. // For platforms with INS fallback, DOP drives accuracy degradation, not operability.

3.3 — Cloud Ceiling Derivation from Layered Coverage

The Open-Meteo API returns separate low, mid, and high cloud cover percentages rather than a single ceiling value. IWEDA derives an approximate ceiling AGL from these layered values using a heuristic model calibrated against METAR-reported ceilings in the development dataset.

// CLOUD CEILING FROM LAYERED COVERAGE — PIECEWISE HEURISTIC // API provides: cloud_cover_low (0–2km), cloud_cover_mid (2–6km), cloud_cover_high (6km+)
IF cloud_low > 50%: ceil = max(300, 6500 - (cloud_low - 50) × 120) // ft AGL // At cloud_low = 100%: ceil ≈ 500ft. At cloud_low = 50%: ceil = 6500ft. ELSE IF cloud_mid > 50%: ceil = max(6500, 20000 - (cloud_mid - 50) × 270) // ft AGL ELSE IF cloud_high > 50%: ceil = 25000ft // High cirrus — no operational ceiling constraint ELSE: ceil = 25000ft // Clear sky assumption
// Precipitation penalty: cloud base lowers in active precip IF rain_inch > 0: ceil = ceil × 0.80 ceil = round(ceil / 100) × 100 // Round to nearest 100ft (METAR convention) // Validation: this heuristic produces ±500ft accuracy vs METAR-reported ceilings // for the 200+ test cases used during development. Largest errors occur at ceil > 8000ft // where the threshold rarely matters operationally.

3.4 — Precipitation Type Discrimination

Snow and freezing rain impose asymmetric penalties not present for liquid rain: ice contamination of LO coatings (B-2A), reduced rotor efficiency and blade icing (rotary wing), laser beam scatter (LGB seekers), and accelerated GPS signal attenuation. The precipitation type discriminator uses API-sourced temperature and snowfall data.

// PRECIPITATION TYPE LOGIC
IF rain_inch = 0 → NONE IF rain_inch > 0 AND temp_2m < 2°C AND snowfall_cm > 0 → SNOW IF rain_inch > 0 AND temp_2m < 2°C AND snowfall_cm = 0 → FZRA (freezing rain) IF rain_inch > 0 AND snowfall_cm > 0 AND temp_2m ∈ [2, 5) → MIXED ELSE → RAIN
// Snow/FZRA triggers two additional penalties in evalCell(): 1. Rotary wing platforms: wind threshold reduced by 5kt (blade icing degrades authority) 2. LGB munitions (GBU-12, AGM-114): effective rain = rain × 2.0 (scatter amplified) 3. B-2A: hard RED if any precipitation + SNOW/FZRA (LO coating contamination)

3.5 — Per-Hour Forecast Confidence Factor

Each hour in the forecast carries a confidence factor (CF) displayed in column headers. This is distinct from the day-level uncertainty multiplier. CF reflects within-day meteorological variability signals.

// PER-HOUR CONFIDENCE FACTOR FORMULA
CF_base = 97 - (day_index × 8) - (hour_within_day × 0.4) CF = CF_base - (precip_probability > 50% ? 5 : 0) - (gust_kt > 25 ? 3 : 0) - (|cloud_cover_change_vs_prev_hour| > 30% ? 4 : 0) CF = max(35, round(CF)) // Floor at 35% — even Day 5 data has some skill // CF < 65 triggers dark-mode cell colors (deep green/amber/red vs bright) // indicating the assessment carries significant meteorological uncertainty.

§ 05

Known Limitations & Data Gaps

Intellectual honesty requires explicit documentation of what this tool does not know and cannot responsibly claim. The following gaps are prioritized by their potential impact on analytical conclusions.

Critical Framing All ESTIMATED thresholds in IWEDA represent analytically-derived approximations, not operational parameters. Russian and Iranian manufacturers have not published weather operating limits for any of the platforms in this database in open sources. Where thresholds are marked ESTIMATED, they should be understood as reasonable analytical bounds, not verified operational minimums. An intelligence analyst should weight ESTIMATED platform assessments accordingly.
UMPK/FAB-500
Threshold Precision
HIGH IMPACT GAP
Gap: The exact INS drift rate for the UMPK kit is unknown in open sources. This determines how quickly accuracy degrades under GNSS jamming. IWEDA's current 6.0 HDOP threshold is derived from comparative analysis with JDAM's 2.0 threshold scaled for UMPK's inferior inertial components (civilian-grade vs military-grade), but the precise crossover point is unknown. Fighterbomber Telegram (Feb 2025) reported "16 bombs per target" under jamming, suggesting near-total accuracy loss — but this is anecdotal, not statistically characterized.
Improvement Path: ACLED backtesting of strike accuracy vs GNSS environment across hundreds of UMPK strikes, correlated with Ukrainian jamming deployment records.
Geran-2/Shahed-136
Wind-to-Sortie Correlation
HIGH IMPACT GAP
Gap: Ukrainian expert Kryvolap assessed that weather is "70–80% probable" as the driver of attack pauses (RBC-Ukraine, Dec 2024). However, no quantitative statistical analysis of wind speed vs Shahed launch frequency exists in open sources. IWEDA's 30kt wind threshold is engineering-derived (97kt cruise speed, 50hp engine, headwind analysis) rather than empirically validated.
Improvement Path: ACLED armed conflict dataset (which timestamps every attack event with location) matched against Open-Meteo historical weather API for the same coordinates and times. With ~2,000+ documented Geran-2 attack events in Ukraine, this would produce statistically significant wind-to-launch correlation.
Stealth Platform
Weather Minimums (US/RU/CN)
HIGH IMPACT GAP
Gap: The B-2A's low-observable coating rain sensitivity, F-22A's LO maintenance minimums, Su-57's comparable thresholds, and J-20's limits are all classified or simply undocumented in open sources. IWEDA uses general airframe rain limits as conservative proxies, but the actual LO-driven threshold (driven by RAM coating degradation from rain impingement at high speed) could be significantly more or less restrictive.
Improvement Path: None available through open-source research. Would require declassification or leaks.
Iskander-M
EO Seeker Activation Altitude
MEDIUM IMPACT GAP
Gap: The Iskander-M's EO/DSMAC seeker activates during terminal dive. The activation altitude determines what cloud ceiling defeats it. RUSI (Aug 2022) confirmed the EO seeker exists but does not specify activation altitude. IWEDA uses 1,500ft AGL as a conservative estimate based on analogous DSMAC activation profiles (Tomahawk Block III: ~2,000ft), adjusted for Iskander's steeper terminal angle. The true value could be 500–3,000ft.
Improvement Path: Ukrainian military analysis of Iskander impact dispersion correlated with cloud ceiling data at time of strike. Dense cloud = INS-only mode = higher CEP; correlation would reveal approximate EO activation window.
Iranian F-14A
Operational Capability
MEDIUM IMPACT GAP
Gap: The IRIAF F-14A fleet's actual operational readiness is deeply uncertain. IISS Military Balance lists ~40 on inventory but serviceability rate is unknown; some estimates suggest fewer than 10 are mission-capable. The avionics state post-sanctions (40+ years without US parts) makes even the airframe's weather envelope uncertain. IWEDA applies conservative VFR-limited thresholds as a reasonable bound but acknowledges the fleet may be too degraded to represent a credible threat in the threshold scenarios depicted.
Improvement Path: Iranian aviation community open-source reporting, IRGC AF official communications, satellite imagery of IRIAF bases for maintenance activity.
Ceiling Calculation
Heuristic Accuracy
LOW IMPACT GAP
Gap: The cloud ceiling heuristic (§3.3) produces ±500ft accuracy vs METAR-reported ceilings for ceilings below 8,000ft. Above 8,000ft, where the heuristic matters less operationally, accuracy degrades to ±2,000ft. The Open-Meteo API does not natively return cloud base height AGL — only cloud cover percentages by layer.
Improvement Path: Pull real METAR data from aviationweather.gov API using nearest ICAO station to target coordinates. This would replace the derived ceiling with measured, station-reported ceiling, resolving the accuracy gap entirely.
ACLED Backtesting
Phase 2 Pipeline
PLANNED ENHANCEMENT
Description: The most powerful methodology improvement available is empirical backtesting of platform operational envelopes using combat event data. The process:
(1) Extract ACLED event records for specific weapon type with timestamps and coordinates.
(2) Query Open-Meteo historical weather API for exact coordinates and hours of each event.
(3) Build a statistical distribution of meteorological conditions present during observed operations.
(4) Derive empirical thresholds from the distribution (e.g., 95th percentile wind speed present during Geran-2 launches = operational wind limit).
This would replace ESTIMATED confidence tags with OBSERVED tags for Tier 1 platforms (Shahed-136/Geran-2, Kalibr, Iskander, FAB-500 UMPK) where hundreds of events are available. It represents the most rigorous open-source methodology achievable without classified data access.
